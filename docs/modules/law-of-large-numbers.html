The Law of Large Numbers

Flip a coin and watch randomness give way to predictability

With a fair coin, we expect 50% heads. But flip it only 10 times and you might see 70% or 30%. Flip it 10,000 times and the result will be remarkably close to 50%. This is the Law of Large Numbers — as sample size grows, the observed average converges to the true average.

Why this matters for A/B testing

The single trial chart shows why you shouldn't peek at early results — they're noisy. The distribution chart shows something deeper: even with a fixed sample size, each experiment gives a different answer. The histogram is the sampling distribution — the foundation of every p-value and confidence interval you'll ever compute.

From Coins to A/B Tests

Coin flips are a clean illustration, but the Law of Large Numbers governs every A/B test you run. Early in an experiment, observed conversion rates and lifts are noisy — just like the first few coin flips. As visitors accumulate day by day, the observed metrics converge to their true values.

Key Takeaway

Whether you're flipping coins or measuring conversion rates, the principle is the same: small samples lie, large samples converge. The multi-experiment chart makes this visceral — in the first few days, experiments with zero true lift can look like big winners or losers. This is why we set minimum sample sizes and don't peek at early results.
