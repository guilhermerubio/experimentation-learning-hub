Hypothesis Testing

Is this coin fair? Build intuition for p-values and statistical significance

Imagine someone hands you a coin and claims it's fair. You flip it 20 times and get 15 heads. Suspicious? The p-value tells you: "If the coin really were fair, how likely is a result this extreme?"

This is exactly how A/B tests work. We assume no effect (the null hypothesis), collect data, and ask whether the result is surprising enough to reject that assumption.

Why this matters for A/B testing

When a coin is fair (no real effect), about alpha % of tests will still appear significant — these are false positives. This is the fundamental tradeoff of hypothesis testing: the significance level you choose directly controls how often you'll be fooled by noise.

Try setting the bias to 0.50 and running 100 tests. Count how many are "significant." That's your false positive rate — and it should be close to alpha.
